Museum of Man: Wayfinding

Challenge

My team and I were presented with the challenge to improve the wayfinding system at the Museum of Man in San Diego.

We discovered two main pain points patrons faced: the museum's map design and lobby space.

About

Role: UX Researcher and Prototyper

Tools: Sketch, pencil and paper, crafting tools (foam board, exacto knife, etc)

Skills: ethnography, user research, prototyping

General Research

User Interviews and Empathizing

We started with interviewing patrons outside the museum, asking them questions such as the reason for their visit and their experiences navigating through the exhibits. To further empathize with the patrons and understand their pain points, we took on the role of common personas we saw in our interviews:

  • Father and child, emulated by having a stroller

  • Visitors with poor eyesight (accessibility)

  • First-time visitors

  • Museum enthusiasts

  • Visitors with maps vs no maps

    • Map Redesign

    The current map design elicited feedback from patrons which we translated into pain points:

  • Legibility: Text on the map was too small, causing illegibility issues.

  • Identification of exhibits: Patrons had a hard time identifying certain exhibits and distinguishing them from each other.

  • Icons: There was some confusion when interpreting icons used for bathrooms, elevators, and stairs.

    • In addition, we conducted competitive analysis and looked into how other museums and theme parks represented this information on their maps. From our findings, we started parallel prototyping our first round of map redesigns.

    Round #1 Prototypes

    We used the original design as a control and made redesigns based on different elements:

  • Color-coded exhibits

  • Labels and descriptions

  • Visual representations of exhibits

  • Orientation of floors

  • Icons of stairs, elevators, and bathroom


    • To test our maps, we came up with 3 contextual and task-based activities for the museum patrons. We used this data to inform our decisions for the next round of map prototypes where we posed the question: what information is most important?

    Round #2 Prototypes

    Map 4: Includes icons + labels, which we found were most effective from our testing.

    Map 5: Incorporates the same elements from Map 4 in a 3D layout, which we drew inspiration for from our competitive analysis

    Final Product

    For our final iteration, we made paper prototypes to lay out the map's information architecture. We then used guerrilla interviewing techniques to get users to think aloud and capture their gut reactions, and used this input for our final map design.
